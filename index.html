Elementary, said he!




Over a hundred years, the enigmatic Sherlock Holmes has eclipsed other detectives as the detective who has singlehandedly paved his own genre of forensic investigation. As a "consulting detective" in the stories. From preserving crime scenes and focusing on fingerprints, blood, and ballistics, Holmes was about 120 years ahead of his time, searching for minute traces of evidence beyond what the naked eye would notice. Sherlock Holmes was the first of his kind to emphasize the importance of an uncontaminated crime scene during police investigation, putting more effort into the facts of a case, rather than accepting what appears to be true.

Character

“My name is Sherlock Holmes. It is my business to know what other people do not know.”
― Arthur Conan Doyle, The Adventure of the Blue Carbuncle - a Sherlock Holmes Short Story

Debuting in A Study in Scarlet in 1887, Arthur Conan Doyle's depiction of Sherlock Holmes captivated audiences with his abiliy to reach targeted conclusions based on minute observations. While not the first detective of his kind, Holmes has become synonymous with forensic science and logical reasoning, becoming the quintessenial renaissance man of looking beyond the surface to the heart of any case.

"There is nothing more deceptive than an obvious fact."
- Sherlock Holmes in The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes: The Boscombe Valley Mystery.

Works

Canonical works that Holmes appeared in:

    Novels
  • A Study in Scarlet (published November 1887 in Beeton's Christmas Annual)
  • The Sign of the Four (published February 1890 in Lippincott's Monthly Magazine)
  • The Hound of the Baskervilles (serialised 1901–1902 in The Strand)
  • The Valley of Fear (serialised 1914–1915 in The Strand)

    • Short story collections
    The short stories, originally published in magazines, were later collected in five anthologies:

  • The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes (stories published 1891–1892 in The Strand)
  • The Memoirs of Sherlock Holmes (stories published 1892–1893 in The Strand)
  • The Return of Sherlock Holmes (stories published 1903–1904 in The Strand)
  • His Last Bow: Some Later Reminiscences of Sherlock Holmes (stories published 1908–1917)
  • The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes (stories published 1921–1927)

Media

With over 75 actors taking part of more than 250 productions internationally, Sherlock Holmes has earned his place in the Guinness World Records as the most portrayed literary human character in film and television history.

Notable actors that make up this list include Michael Caine, Robert Downey Jr., Ian McKellan, Johnny Depp, Will Ferrell, Benedict Cumberbatch, and Johnny Lee Miller.
